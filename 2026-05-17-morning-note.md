## Macro Backdrop

Inflation delivered another shock to markets this week. April CPI came in at **3.8% year-over-year** — the hottest reading since May 2023 — with core CPI rising **+0.4% month-over-month** and **+2.8% annually**, still well above the Federal Reserve's 2% target. Energy was the primary accelerant, with gasoline prices surging **+28.4% YoY** and fuel oil skyrocketing **+54.3% YoY**, directly reflecting the Strait of Hormuz supply shock now entering its third month. The PPI print that preceded CPI was similarly alarming, and the 10-year Treasury yield spiked to **4.60%** — a fresh one-year high — while the 30-year climbed to **5.12%** as bond investors demanded a steeper term premium. The U.S. government sold a staggering **$691 billion** in Treasury securities in a single week, reinforcing concerns about fiscal supply overwhelming buyer appetite and pressuring duration across the curve.

The Federal Reserve, now under newly confirmed Chair **Kevin Warsh** (sworn in May 14 following a narrow 54-45 Senate vote), has held its benchmark rate steady at **3.50%–3.75%** since December. But the policy calculus has shifted decisively. Markets have priced out all rate cuts through end-2027, and futures now assign a **>50% probability of at least one rate hike before year-end 2026**, with a full hike fully priced by March 2027. Warsh is a known inflation hawk; his first public remarks this week will be closely parsed for any signal on the Fed's tolerance for reaccelerating prices. The risk of a policy error — moving too early into a rate hike in a slowing consumer environment — is not trivial.

Oil is the linchpin of the entire macro picture. **Brent crude settled near $108/bbl** on Friday, up **~8% on the week**; **WTI settled near $106/bbl**, up **~11% on the week**. The Strait of Hormuz remains effectively closed following the escalation of the U.S.-Iran conflict, and the IEA estimates that crude and fuel flows through the Strait fell by approximately **4 million barrels per day** in March and April. The agency warns global oil markets could remain materially undersupplied through October even if a resolution is reached next month. The EIA projects Brent at ~$106/bbl through June, with global inventories drawing at **8.5 million b/d** in 2Q26. The energy supply shock simultaneously drives inflation higher and compresses margins for industrials, transport, and consumer discretionary — a structurally stagflationary configuration.

## Trade Ideas

**1. Long Energy Producers — XOM / CVX / XLE**
With the Strait of Hormuz closure entering its third month and the IEA projecting a ~8.5 million b/d global inventory draw through 2Q26, Brent anchored near $106–$110/bbl is not a transient spike — it is a structural supply dislocation. Integrated majors Exxon Mobil (XOM) and Chevron (CVX) generate significant free cash flow at current prices, sustaining buybacks and dividends even in a modest price pullback scenario. The XLE ETF offers diversified sector exposure. Entry near current levels with a stop near $95 Brent represents an attractive risk/reward into a supply-constrained summer. Primary risk: a credible Hormuz ceasefire deal triggering rapid oil-price mean-reversion.

**2. Short Long-Duration Treasuries — TBT (2× Short 20+ Yr Treasury)**
The 30-year at 5.12% and 10-year at 4.60% may not be the ceiling. The combination of a reaccelerating CPI, a hawkish new Fed Chair establishing credibility, and $691 billion in weekly Treasury supply creates a materially negative supply/demand dynamic for long bonds. Markets now price a >50% probability of a rate hike before year-end. TBT provides leveraged exposure to further curve steepening — treat as a tactical trade with defined position sizing given the instrument's convexity. Risk: a geopolitical de-escalation shock causing a flight-to-safety bid into Treasuries.

**3. Defensive Rotation — Consumer Staples (XLP)**
In a stagflationary environment with slowing consumer spending, rising rates, and deteriorating equity breadth, consumer staples offer a natural harbor. Companies with durable pricing power — Procter & Gamble (PG), Coca-Cola (KO), Colgate-Palmolive (CL) — can pass commodity cost increases through to end consumers more effectively than discretionary names. XLP has underperformed during the prior growth rally, offering relative value as rotation accelerates. The dividend cushion also provides partial insulation from rising yield headwinds.

**4. Long Oil Services — SLB / HAL**
A prolonged Hormuz disruption and elevated crude prices are directly incentivizing E&P capital expenditure expansion — and oil services are the primary beneficiary. Schlumberger (SLB) and Halliburton (HAL) carry diversified international revenue bases that partially offset Middle East concentration risk. SLB in particular benefits from secular demand for digital oilfield technology, which is becoming standard across new development projects. With Brent remaining elevated, E&P capex budgets should be revised upward in 2H26, driving incremental demand for drilling, completions, and production services.
