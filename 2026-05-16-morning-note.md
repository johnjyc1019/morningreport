## Macro Backdrop

April's CPI report delivered a significant shock to markets this week, with headline consumer prices rising 3.8% year-over-year — the hottest reading since May 2023 and well above consensus expectations. Core CPI, which strips out volatile food and energy components, came in at +2.8% annually and +0.4% month-over-month, confirming that inflationary pressures are not isolated to energy and remain stubbornly above the Federal Reserve's 2% target. The primary culprit is energy: gasoline prices surged 28.4% year-over-year and fuel oil spiked 54.3%, reflecting both the ongoing US-Iran conflict and an effective standstill in traffic through the Strait of Hormuz.

The bond market has moved aggressively to reprice the rate outlook. The 10-year Treasury yield climbed to 4.60% on Friday — a fresh one-year high — while the 30-year breached 5.12%, levels last seen before the Fed's 2024 easing cycle began. The CME FedWatch tool now prices virtually zero probability of rate cuts through end-2027, a complete reversal from the three cuts markets expected entering the year. More strikingly, traders are now assigning better than 50% odds to at least one rate *hike* by the Fed before year-end 2026. The Fed's benchmark overnight rate remains at 3.50%–3.75%, unchanged since December, but Chair Powell and colleagues face mounting pressure as the second inflation wave takes hold.

Crude oil has become the central macro variable. Brent and WTI are trading near their highest levels of 2026 — up approximately 80% year-to-date and 60% since the outbreak of US-Iran hostilities — making energy the dominant force reshaping inflation expectations, consumer spending power, and central bank optionality globally. While equity markets staged a strong April recovery (S&P 500 reclaimed its post-conflict highs) on tentative de-escalation signals, the renewed move higher in yields and oil is renewing risk-off sentiment heading into the weekend.

## Trade Ideas

**1. Long Energy Infrastructure (XLE / Midstream MLPs)**
Oil at 2026 highs and the Strait of Hormuz disruption have structurally elevated energy cash flows for domestic producers and pipeline operators. Policy is explicitly shifting toward domestic energy generation, grids, and storage — a multi-year capex supercycle. With WTI/Brent near year highs and no near-term resolution to the Iran conflict visible, energy remains the sector with the most defensible earnings revisions. Prefer integrated majors and US LNG infrastructure plays with take-or-pay contract structures that insulate against spot price volatility.

**2. Short Duration / Long Volatility on Rates (TBT / SRVIX)**
The second wave of inflation has effectively ended the rate-cut narrative and introduced credible probability of hikes. With the 10-year at 4.60% and 30-year at 5.12%, and the Fed pinned on hold, the asymmetry favors further yield normalization — particularly if May CPI (due next month) confirms the trend. Short duration via inverse Treasury ETFs or expressed through rate vol offers a hedge against a policy error scenario where the Fed is forced to hike into a slowing economy. Position sizing should reflect the binary nature of the Iran conflict resolution risk.

**3. Long Defense / Aerospace (ITA / LMT / RTX)**
The US-Iran conflict entering its third month with no resolution, combined with Europe's accelerating defense spending commitments, creates a durable demand backdrop for defense primes. Karman's miss was largely noise (a 1-cent shortfall); the structural backlog build across aerospace and defense is intact. Lockheed Martin and RTX trade at modest multiples relative to their order pipelines and benefit from cost-plus contracting that insulates margins from broader input cost inflation.

**4. Underweight Consumer Discretionary (XLY Short / Pair vs. XLP)**
Birkenstock's –5.5% miss reaction illustrates the fragility of premium consumer brands in a real-income-squeeze environment. Gasoline at multi-year highs is a direct drag on discretionary spending — effectively a regressive tax on middle-income households. With rates pricing out cuts and consumer credit delinquencies likely rising, the consumer discretionary sector faces a challenging combination of margin pressure and demand softening. A long Staples / short Discretionary pair trade offers defensive positioning without full market beta removal.
